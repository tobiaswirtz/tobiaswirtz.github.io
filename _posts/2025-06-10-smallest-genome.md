In the past few months, I have been spending quite a bit of time reading about genetics. At school, I was determinedly disinterested in biology on account of not being thrilled about having to study (sorry Mrs Srouji!). What has kindled my interest is the complexity of genomes. A genome is the set of all genes of that make up the species. The human genome contains around 3.4 billion base pairs which encode about 30 000 proteins[^1]. Each base pair is 2 bits, therefore the human genome is about 6.8 Gigabits or 0.85 Gigabytes. That sounds remarkably information-efficient already when you consider that this contains all the information needed to build and grow a human. However if you fill an Excel sheet with 30 000 random 3-letter strings (which is enough to encode around 150k proteins) and export it as a CSV file, you only get a file of about 150 Kilobytes. Information-theoretically speaking you could encode all the 30 000 proteins required by the human body in about 0.02% of the human genome! Suddenly it seems like the human genome is incredibly wasteful. 

One could think that since humans are clearly the most able form of life on the planet, that should mean that our genome would contain significantly more information measured in base paris compared to other species. This is not true. There are animals[^2] and plants[^3] with genome sizes that are multipes of ours. 

So what's going on here? The human genome seems to not be providing as much information as it could provide while it also isn't even the largest on the block? What gives? This will be the first of a series of posts that will try to document my learning path to answer this question.

To start, we should probably not look at the complexity of a human, but start out with something much more simple - the _minimum viable genome_ if you will.

## Our specimen
By "minimum viable" I mean a genome that contains all the information for life to exist independently, that is, not outsourcing any of its vital functions to other organisms or cells.
